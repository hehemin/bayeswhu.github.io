Overview

Overview

  • This lecture illustrates two of the most important theorems of probability and statistics: The law of large numbers (LLN) and the central limit theorem (CLT)

  • These beautiful theorems lie behind many of the most fundamental results in econometrics and quantitative economic modeling

  • The lecture is based around simulations that show the LLN and CLT in action

  • We also demonstrate how the LLN and CLT break down when the assumptions they are based on do not hold

  • In addition, we examine several useful extensions of the classical theorems, such as
    • The delta method, for smooth functions of random variables
    • The multivariate case

  • Some of these extensions are presented as exercises

Relationships

Relationships

  • The CLT refines the LLN

  • The LLN gives conditions under which sample moments converge to population moments as sample size increases

  • The CLT provides information about the rate at which sample moments converge to population moments as sample size increases

LLN

The Classical LLN

  • The classical law of large numbers concerns independent and identically distributed (IID) random variables

  • Here is the strongest version of the classical LLN, known as Kolmogorov's strong law

  • Let \(X_1,...,X_n\) be independent and identically distributed scalar random variables, with common distribution \(F\)

  • When it exists, let \(\mu\) denote the common mean of this sample: \[ \mu=\mathbb{E}X=\int xF(dx) \]

The Classical LLN

  • In addition, let \[ \bar{X}_n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \]

  • Kolmogorov's strong law states that, if \(\mathbb{E}|X|\) is finite, then \[ \mathbb{P}\{\bar{X}_n\to\mu \text{ as }n\to\infty\}=1 \]

  • What does this last expression mean?

The Classical LLN

  • Let's think about it from a simulation perspective, imagining for a moment that our computer can generate perfect random samples (which of course it can't)

  • Let's also imagine that we can generate infinite sequences, so that the statement \(\bar{X}_n\to\mu\) can be evaluated

  • In this setting, the equation should be interpreted as meaning that the probability of the computer producing a sequence where \(\bar{X}_n\to\mu\) fails to occur is zero

Proof

  • The proof of Kolmogorov's strong law is nontrivial

  • On the other hand, we can prove a weaker version of the LLN very easily and still get most of the intuition

  • The version we prove is as follows: If \(X_1,...,X_n\) is IID with \(\mathbb{E}X^2_i<\infty\), then, for any \(\varepsilon>0\), we have \[ \mathbb{P}\{|\bar{X}_n-\mu|\geq\varepsilon\}\to 0 \text{ as } n\to\infty \]

  • (This version is weaker because we claim only convergence in probability rather than almost sure convergence, and assume a finite second moment)

Proof

  • To see that this is so, fix \(\varepsilon>0\), and let \(\sigma^2\) be the variance of each \(X_i\)

  • Recall the Chebyshev inequality, which tells us that \[ \mathbb{P}\{|\bar{X}_n-\mu|\geq\varepsilon\}\leq\frac{\mathbb{E}[(\bar{X}_n-\mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2} \]

Proof

  • Now ovserve that \[ \begin{array}[lll] \mathbb{E}[(\bar{X}_n-\mu)^2] & = & \mathbb{E}\{[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\mu)]^2\} \\ & = & \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n\mathbb{E}(X_i-\mu)(X_j-\mu) \\ & = & \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n\mathbb{E}(X_i-\mu)^2 \\ & = & \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \end{array} \]

Proof

  • Here the crucial step is at the third equality, which follows from independence

  • Independence means that if \(i\neq j\), then the covariance term \(\mathbb{E}(X_ia\mu)(X_ja\mu)\) drops out

  • As a result, \(n^2an\) terms vanish, leading us to a final expression that goes to zero in \(n\)

  • Combining our last result with Chebyshev inequality, we come to the estimate \[ \mathbb{P}\{|\bar{X}_n-\mu|\geq\varepsilon\}\leq\frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \]

  • The claim is now clear

Proof

  • Of course, if the sequence \(X_1,...,X_n\) is correlated, then the cross-product terms \(\mathbb{E}(X_ia\mu)(X_ja\mu)\) are not necessarily zero

  • While this doesn't mean that the same line of argument is impossible, it does mean that if we want a similar result then the covariances should be aalmost zeroa for amosta of these terms

  • In a long sequence, this would be true if, for example, \(\mathbb{E}(X_ia\mu)(X_ja\mu)\) approached zero when the difference between \(i\) and \(j\) became large

  • In other words, the LLN can still work if the sequence \(X_1,...,X_n\) has a kind of aasymptotic independencea, in the sense that correlation falls to zero as variables become further apart in the sequence

  • This idea is very important in time series analysis, and we'll come across it again soon enough

  • What happens if the condition \(\mathbb{E}|X|<\infty\) in the statement of the LLN is not satisfied?

  • This might be the case if the underlying distribution is heavy tailed a the best known example is the Cauchy distribution, which has density \[ f(x)=\frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}\:(x\in\mathbb{R}) \]

  • The next figure shows 100 independent draws from this distribution

Infinite Mean

  • Notice how extreme observations are far more prevalent here than the previous figure

  • Let's now have a look at the behavior of the sample mean

Infinite Mean

  • Here we've increased \(n\) to 1000, but the sequence still shows no sign of converging

  • Will convergence become visible if we take \(n\) even larger?

  • The answer is no

  • To see this, recall that the characteristic function of the Cauchy distribution is \[ \phi(t)=\mathbb{E}e^{itX}=\int e^{itx}f(x)dx=e^{-|t|} \]

Infinite Mean

  • Using independence, the characteristic function of the sample mean becomes \[ \begin{array}[lll] \mathbb{E}e^{it\bar{X}_n} & = & \mathbb{E}\exp\{i\frac{t}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n X_j\} \\ & = & \mathbb{E}\prod_{j=1}^n\exp\{i\frac{t}{n}X_j\} \\ & = & \prod_{j=1}^n\mathbb{E}\exp\{i\frac{t}{n}X_j\}=[\phi(t/n)]^n \end{array} \]

  • In view of original characteristic function, this is just \(e^{a|t|}\)

Infinite Mean

  • Thus, in the case of the Cauchy distribution, the sample mean itself has the very same Cauchy distribution, regardless of \(n\)

  • In particular, the sequence \(\bar{X}_n\) does not converge to a point

CLT

Statement of the Theorem

  • The central limit theorem is one of the most remarkable results in all of mathematics

  • In the classical IID setting, it tells us the following:

  • If the sequence \(X_1,...,X_n\) is IID, with common mean \(\mu\) and common variance \(\sigma^2\in(0,\infty)\), then \[ \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_na\mu)\overset{d}{\to}N(0,\sigma^2)\text{ as }na\infty \]

  • Here \(\overset{d}{\to}N(0,\sigma^2)\) indicates convergence in distribution to a centered (i.e, zero mean) normal with standard deviation \(\sigma\)

Intuition

  • The striking implication of the CLT is that for any distribution with finite second moment, the simple operation of adding independent copies always leads to a Gaussian curve

  • A relatively simple proof of the central limit theorem can be obtained by working with characteristic functions

  • The proof is elegant but almost anticlimactic, and it provides surprisingly little intuition

  • In fact all of the proofs of the CLT that we know are similar in this respect

  • Why does adding independent copies produce a bell-shaped distribution?

  • Part of the answer can be obtained by investigating addition of independent Bernoulli random variables

Intuition

  • In particular, let \(X_i\) be binary, with \(\mathbb{P}\{X_i=0\}=\mathbb{P}\{X_i=1\}=0.5\), and let \(X_1,...,X_n\) be independent

  • Think of \(X_i=1\) as a asuccessa, so that \(Y_n=\sum^n_{i=1}X_i\) is the number of successes in \(n\) trials

  • The next figure plots the probability mass function of \(Y_n\) for \(n=1,2,4,8\)

Intuition

Intuition

  • When \(n=1\), the distribution is flat a one success or no successes have the same probability

  • When \(n=2\) we can either have 0, 1 or 2 successes

  • Notice the peak in probability mass at the mid-point \(k=1\)

  • The reason is that there are more ways to get 1 success (afail then succeeda or asucceed then faila) than to get zero or two successes

  • Moreover, the two trials are independent, so the outcomes afail then succeeda and asucceed then faila are just as likely as the outcomes afail then faila and asucceed then succeeda

  • Here, already we have the essence of the CLT: addition under independence leads probability mass to pile up in the middle and thin out at the tails

Intuition

  • For \(n=4\) and \(n=8\) we again get a peak at the amiddlea value (halfway between the minimum and the maximum possible value)

  • The intuition is the same a there are simply more ways to get these middle outcomes

  • If we continue, the bell-shaped curve becomes ever more pronounced

  • We are witnessing the binomial approximation of the normal distribution

Simulation 1

  • Since the CLT seems almost magical, running simulations that verify its implications is one good way to build intuition

  • To this end, we now perform the following simulation
    1. Choose an arbitrary distribution \(F\) for the underlying observations \(X_i\)
    2. Generate independent draws of \(Y_n=\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_na\mu)\)
    3. Use these draws to compute some measure of their distribution a such as a histogram
    4. Compare the latter to \(N(0,\sigma^2)\)

  • Here's some code that does exactly this for the exponential distribution \(F(x)=1ae^{a\lambda x}\)

  • Please experiment with other choices of F, but remember that, to conform with the conditions of the CLT, the distribution must have finite second moment

Simulation 1

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import expon, norm
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from matplotlib import rc
# == Set parameters == #
n = 250     # Choice of n
k = 100000  # Number of draws of Y_n
distribution = expon(2)  # Exponential distribution, lambda = 1/2
mu, s = distribution.mean(), distribution.std()
# == Draw underlying RVs. Each row contains a draw of X_1,..,X_n == #
data = distribution.rvs((k, n))
# == Compute mean of each row, producing k draws of \bar X_n == #
sample_means = data.mean(axis=1)
# == Generate observations of Y_n == #
Y = np.sqrt(n) * (sample_means - mu)

Simulation 1

# == Plot == #
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
xmin, xmax = -3 * s, 3 * s
ax.set_xlim(xmin, xmax)
ax.hist(Y, bins=60, alpha=0.5, normed=True)
xgrid = np.linspace(xmin, xmax, 200)
ax.plot(xgrid, norm.pdf(xgrid, scale=s), 'k-', lw=2, label=r'$N(0, \sigma^2)$')
ax.legend()
plt.show()

The Multivariate Case

  • The law of large numbers and central limit theorem work just as nicely in multidimensional settings

  • To state the results, let's recall some elementary facts about random vectors

  • A random vector \(\mathbf{X}\) is just a sequence of \(k\) random variables \((\mathbf{X_1},...,\mathbf{X_k})\)

  • Each realization of \(\mathbf{X}\) is an element of \(\mathbb{R}^k\)

  • A collection of random vectors \((\mathbf{X_1},...,\mathbf{X_n})\) is called independent if, given any \(n\) vectors \((\mathbf{x_1},...,\mathbf{x_n})\) in \(\mathbb{R}^k\), we have \[ \mathbb{P}\{\mathbf{X_1}\leq\mathbf{x_1},...,\mathbf{X_n}\leq\mathbf{x_n}\}=\mathbb{P}\{\mathbf{X_1}\leq\mathbf{x_1}\}\times...\times\mathbb{P}\{\mathbf{X_n}\leq\mathbf{x_n}\} \]

  • The vector inequality \(\mathbf{X}\leq\mathbf{x}\) means that \(X_j\leq x_j\) for \(j=1,...,k\)

The Multivariate Case

  • Let \(\mu_j=\mathbb{E}[X_j]\) for all \(j=1,...,k\)

  • The expectation \(\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}]\) of \(\mathbf{X}\) is defined to be the vector of expectations: \[ \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}]=\begin{pmatrix} \mathbb{E}[X_1] \ \mathbb{E}[X_2] \ \vdots \ \mathbb{E}[X_k] \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} \mu_1 \ \mu_2 \ \vdots \ \mu_k \ \end{pmatrix}=\mathbf{\mu} \]

  • The variance-covariance matrix of random vector \(\mathbf{X}\) is defined as \[ Var[\mathbf{X}]=\mathbb{E}[(\mathbf{X-\mu})(\mathbf{X-\mu})'] \]

The Multivariate Case

  • Expanding this out, we get \[ Var[\mathbf{X}]=\begin{pmatrix} \mathbb{E}[(X_1-\mu_1)(X_1-\mu_1)] & ... & \mathbb{E}[(X_1-\mu_1)(X_k-\mu_k)] \\ \mathbb{E}[(X_2-\mu_2)(X_1-\mu_1)] & ... & \mathbb{E}[(X_2-\mu_2)(X_k-\mu_k)] \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \mathbb{E}[(X_k-\mu_k)(X_1-\mu_1)] & ... & \mathbb{E}[(X_k-\mu_k)(X_k-\mu_k)] \end{pmatrix} \]

  • The \(j,k\)-th term is the scalar covariance between \(X_j\) and \(X_k\)

  • With this notation we can proceed to the multivariate LLN and CLT

The Multivariate Case

  • Let \((\mathbf{X_1},...,\mathbf{X_n})\) be a sequence of independent and identically distributed random vectors, each one taking values in \(\mathbb{R}^k\)

  • Let \(\mathbf{\mu}\) be the vector \(\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X_i}]\), and let \(\mathbf{\Sigma}\) be the variance-covariance matrix of \(\mathbf{X_i}\)

  • Interpreting vector addition and scalar multiplication in the usual way (i.e., pointwise), let \[ \mathbf{\bar{X}_n}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\mathbf{X_i} \]

The Multivariate Case

  • In this setting, the LLN tells us that \[ \mathbb{P}\{\mathbf{\bar{X}_n}\to\mathbf{\mu}\text{ as }n\to\infty\}=1 \]

  • Here \(\mathbf{\bar{X}_n}\to\mathbf{\mu}\) means that \(\left\|\mathbf{\bar{X}_n}\to\mathbf{\mu}\right\|\to 0\), where \(\left\|a\right\|\) is the standard Euclidean norm

  • The CLT tells us that, provided \(\mathbf{\Sigma}\) is finite, \[ \sqrt{n}\mathbf{\bar{X}_n-\mu} \overset{d}{\to}N(\mathbf{0,\Sigma})\text{ as }n\to\infty \]

Exercises 1

  • One very useful consequence of the central limit theorem is as follows

  • Assume the conditions of the CLT as stated above

  • If \(g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is differentiable at \(\mu\) and \(g'(\mu)\neq 0\), then \[ \sqrt{n}\{g(\bar{X}_n)ag(\mu)\} \overset{d}{\to} N(0,g'(\mu)^2\sigma^2) \text{ as }n\to\infty \]

  • This theorem is used frequently in statistics to obtain the asymptotic distribution of estimators a many of which can be expressed as functions of sample means

  • These kinds of results are often said to use the adelta methoda

  • The proof is based on a Taylor expansion of \(g\) around the point \(\mu\)

Exercises 2

  • In a statistical setting, one often wants the right hand side to be standard normal, so that confidence intervals are easily computed

  • This normalization can be achieved on the basis of three observations

  • First, if \(\mathbf{X}\) is a random vector in \(\mathbb{R}^k\) and \(\mathbf{A}\) is constant and \(k\times k\), then \[ Var[\mathbf{AX}]=\mathbf{A}Var[\mathbf{X}]\mathbf{A'} \]

  • Second, by the continuous mapping theorem, if \(\mathbf{Z_n} \overset{d}{\to} \mathbf{Z}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^k\) and \(\mathbf{A}\) is constant and \(k\times k\), then \[ \mathbf{AZ_n} \overset{d}{\to} \mathbf{AZ} \]

Exercises 2

  • Third, if \(\mathbf{S}\) is a \(k\times k\) symmetric positive definite matrix, then there exists a symmetric positive definite matrix \(\mathbf{Q}\), called the inverse square root of \(\mathbf{S}\), such that \[ \mathbf{QSQ'=I} \]

  • Here \(\mathbf{I}\) is the \(k\times k\) identity matrix

  • Putting these things together, your first exercise is to show that if \(\mathbf{Q}\) is the inverse square root of \(\mathbf{\Sigma}\), then \[ \mathbf{Z_n}=\sqrt{n}\mathbf{Q(\bar{X}_n-\mu)} \overset{d}{\to} \mathbf{Z\sim N(0,I)} \]

Exercises 2

  • Applying the continuous mapping theorem one more time tells us that \[ \left\|\mathbf{Z_n}\right\|^2 \overset{d}{\to} \left\|\mathbf{Z}\right\|^2 \]

  • Given the distribution of \(\mathbf{Z}\), we conclude that \[ n\left\|\mathbf{Q(\bar{X}_n-\mu)}\right\|^2 \overset{d}{\to} \chi^2(k) \] where \(\chi^2(k)\) is the chi-squared distribution with \(k\) degrees of freedom

  • (Recall that \(k\) is the dimension of \(\mathbf{X_i}\), the underlying random vectors)
